1. Definitions

1.1. Postulate/Axiom

A statement which is accepted even without proof.

1.2. Theorem

A statement which can only be true when it is proven.

1.3. Undefined Terms

There are three undefined terms: point, line, and plane.

•

A point denotes a position in space and has no dimension.

•

A line is a series of points that extends in opposite directions without end.

•

A plane is a flat surface.

1.4. Segment

A segment is the part of a line consisting of one endpoint and all the points of the line on one side of the endpoint.

1.5. Ray

A ray is the part of a line consisting of one endpoint and all the points of the line on one side of the endpoint.

1.6. Opposite Rays

Opposite rays are two collinear rays with the same endpoint. Opposite rays always form a line.

1.10. Collinear Points

A set of points is collinear if there is a line which contains all the points of the set.

1.11. Coplanar Points

A set of points is coplanar if there is a plane which contains all the points of the set.

1.12. Non-collinear Points

A set of points that do not lie on the same line and plane

1.13. Space

A set of all points

1.14. Convex

A set M is called convex if, for every two points P and Q of the set, the entire segment lies in M

1.15. Angle

If two rays have the same end point, but do not lie on the same line, then their union is an angle. The two rays are called its sides, and their common point is called its vertex.

2. Postulates

2.1. Postulate 1: The Distance Postulate

To every pair of different points there corresponds a unique positive number.

2.2. Postulate 2: The Ruler Postulate

The points of a line can be placed in correspondence with real numbers in such a way that:

1

To every point there corresponds exactly one real number;

2

To every real number there corresponds exactly one point of the line; and

3

The distance between any points is the absolute value of the difference of the corresponding numbers

2.3. Postulate 3: The Ruler Placement Postulate

Given two points P and Q of a line, the coordinate system can be chosen in such a way that the coordinate of P is zero and the coordinate of Q is positive.

2.4. Postulate 4: The Line Postulate

For every two points there is exactly one line that contains both points

2.5. Postulate 5

Every plane contains at least three non-collinear points, and a space contains at least four non-coplanar points.

2.6. Postulate 6

If two points of a line lie in a plane, then the line lies in the same plane.

2.7. Postulate 7: The Plane Postulate

Any three points lie in at least one plane, and any three non-collinear points lie in exactly one plane.

2.8. Postulate 8

If two different planes intersect, then their intersection is a line.

2.9. Postulate 9: Plane Separation Postulate

Given a line and a plane containing it. The points of the plane that do not lie on the line form two sets such that:

1

Each of the sets are convex

2

If P is in one of the sets and Q is in the other, then the segment intersects the line

2.10. Postulate 10: Space Separation Postulate

The points of space that do not lie in a given plane form two sets, such that:

1

Each of the sets are convex

2

If P is in one of the sets and Q is in the other, then the segment intersects the line

2.11. Postulate 11: The Angle Measurement Postulate

To every angle there corresponds a real number between 0 and 180.

2.12. SSS Postulate (Side - Side - Side)

If the sides of one triangle are congruent to the sides of a second triangle, then the triangles are congruent.

2.13. SAS Postulate (Side - Included Angle - Side)

If the two sides and the included angle of one triangle are congruent to the other two sides and included angle of another triangle, then the triangles are congruent.

2.14. ASA Postulate (Angle - Included Side - Angle)

If the two angles and the included side of one triangle are congruent to the other two angles and included side of another triangle, then the triangles are congruent.

2.15. AAS Postulate (Angle - Angle - Non-Included Side)

If the two angles and a non-included side of one triangle are congruent to the two corresponding angles and non-included side of a second triangle, then the triangles are congruent.

If A, B, and C are three different points of the same line, then exactly one of them is between the other two.

Every segment has exactly one midpoint.

3.4. Theorem 4

If a line intersects a plane not containing it, then the intersection contains only one point.

3.5. Theorem 5

If a line intersects a plane not containing it, then the intersection contains only one point.

3.6. Theorem 6

Given a line and a point not on the line, there is exactly one point containing both.

3.7. Theorem 7

If two different planes intersect, then their intersection is a line.

3.8. Theorem 8

Every right angle has measure 90, and every angle with measure 90 is a right angle.

3.9. Theorem 9

Congruence between angles is an equivalence relation.

3.10. Theorem 10

If two angles are complementary, then they are both acute.

3.11. Theorem 11

Any two right angles are congruent

3.12. Theorem 12

If two angles are both congruent and supplementary, then each is a right angle.

3.13. The Supplement Theorem

Supplements of congruent angles are congruent.

3.14. The Complement Theorem

Complements of congruent angles are congruent.

3.15. The Vertical Angle Theorem (VAT)

Vertical angles are congruent.

3.16. Theorem 16

If two intersecting lines form one right angle, then they form four right angles.

3.17. Theorem 17

The perpendicular bisectors of the sides of a triangle are concurrent at a point equidistant from the vertices.

3.18. Theorem 18

The bisectors of the angles of a triangle are concurrent at a point equidistant from the sides.

3.19. Theorem 19

The medians of a triangle intersect in a point that is two-thirds the distance from each vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side.
